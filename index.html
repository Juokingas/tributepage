Muhammad Ali`s 10 greatest fights

Here is the top 10 best Muhammad Ali`s fights

  1. Vs. Tunney Hunsaker - Oct. 29, 1960

    You have to start your career somewhere and against someone. Before he changed his name to Muhammad Ali, he began his boxing career as Cassius Clay. In his amateur days, Clay had a record of 100-5, wonsix KentuckyGolden Glovestitles, two national Golden Gloves titles, anAmateur Athletic UnionNational Title and won a gold medal in the light heavyweight division in the 1960 Summer Olympics. The Hunsaker fight took place in San Antonioand was a six-round affair.Hunsaker was a good test for Clay. He had a 15-9-1 record, so he wasn't a dominant fighter but was a journeyman who could give Clay some fits but would ultimately fall to the Olympian. Clay won the fight by unanimous decision but said in his autobiography that Hunsakerhit him with one of the hardest body shots he ever received.

  2. Vs. Archie Moore - Nov. 15, 1962

    By this point, Clay was 15-0 and buzz was starting to surround the young heavyweight. Now he was about to stepup and face his toughest test to date. Moore is one of the best pound-for-pound boxers of all-timeandhad just come off a 10-year reign as the light heavyweight champion. Moore had been training Clay but he decided to leave his camp in 1960 for refusing to do chores. This is when the bravado of Clay came to light when he proclaimed before the fight,"Archie Moore ...Must fall in four." And fall in four Moore did for Clay's first win over a name opponent.

  3. Vs. Sonny Liston I - Feb. 25, 1964

    Clay won three more fights and people were clamoring for him get a crack at the undisputed heavyweight champion of the world. Liston was coming off two vicious first-round knockouts of former heavyweight champ Floyd Patterson. Not many people gave Clay much of a chance to unseat the 7-1 favorite in Liston. That didn't stop Clay from trash-talking Listonand perhaps his most famous phrase that he would"float like a butterfly and sting like a bee." And that is what Clay did. He was too quick for the powerful Liston, getting in and getting out. Liston had no answer for Clay's speed and power and it proved to be too much as the former champion didn't get off his stool to start the seventh round and Clay became the undisputed heavyweight champion of the world.

  4. Vs. Floyd Patterson - Nov. 22, 1965

    Coming off two devastating knockout losses to then-champion Liston, Patterson won five consecutive fights and was granted an opportunity to become a three-time heavyweight champ. After the first Liston fight, Clay adopted the name Muhammad Ali after converting to Islam and was looking to make his second title defense. Ali won the fight by 12th-round TKO in a fight he controlled throughout. Hewas criticized fornot finishing it sooner and even went as far as not throwing a punch in the first round.

  5. Vs. Karl Mildenberger - Sept. 10, 1966

    This would be the sixth defense of his heavyweight title and Ali decided to travel to Germany for the fight. Mildenberger was a tricky southpaw from Germany who had won numerous European and Continental titlesand would be a tough foe for Ali. There were moments during the fight in which Ali had some difficulty but he pulled through to win by TKO in Round 12.

  6. Vs. Leon Spinks - Sept. 15, 1978

    Ali had lost the title in an upset to Spinksby split decision in February and was granted a chance to reclaim the gold in New Orleans. Ali controlled the entire bout to win by unanimousdecision to takethe title for the third and final time. It ended up being the final win of his career. The fight broke all boxing records for that time. About 90 million people watched the fight on ABC and 63,350 people packed the Superdome, marking thebiggest indoor attendance for a boxing match.

  7. Vs. Jerry Quarry I - Oct. 26, 1970

    This would be Ali's first fight in three-and-half years after refusing to be drafted into the Army for the Vietnam War. Ali fought the case and was denied a license by every state until hewas granted a license in Georgia. Ali defeated Quarry by third-round TKO after Quarry suffered a cut and wasn't allowed to continue. Ali was a little rusty because of the inactivity but the win was a testament to how good Ali was.

  8. Vs. George Foreman - Oct. 30, 1974

    This fight was dubbed the"Rumble in the Jungle" and took place in Zaire. Foreman was 40-0 and considered the hardest puncher in the division. Ali was 32 years old and you had started to see his reflexes and speed slow down. Just about no one gave Ali much of chance. In the second round, Ali decided to go against the ropes and adopted the"Rope-A-Dope" style and it ended up wearing Foreman out halfway through the fight. Ali saw the opening and went to work and knocked out Foreman in the eighth round to win the title for the second time.

  9. Vs. Joe Frazier I - March 8, 1971

    When Ali was on his three-year hiatus, Frazier seized the opportunity and had become the heavyweight champion. When Ali returned it became inevitable they would meet inside the squared circle. The two undefeated champions finally met on that March night in Madison Square Garden and the hype leading into the fight was titled"The Fight of the Century". With the world watching, Frazier proved for that night, he was the better man and won by unanimous decision to hand Ali his first loss.

  10. Vs Frazier III - Oct. 1, 1975

    Ali and Frazier rematched in 1974 with Ali winning an uneventful unanimous decision. You knew a trilogy match would happen; it was just a matter of when it would take place. Not only did it take place, but it went across the globe to Manilla,Philippines. This fight was dubbed the "Thrilla in Manila" with temperatures near 100 degrees.At the time, it was considered the most brutal fight in the history of the heavyweight division. They went toetotoe for 14 rounds with Ali winning the fight asFrazier's trainer, Eddie Futch, stoppedthe fight after the 14th. After the fight, Ali saidthe bout"was the closest thing to dying that I know."
